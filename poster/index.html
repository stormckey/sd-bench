Key Observation

What this setup reveals about suffix decoding

Suffix decoding is generally a bit better than prompt lookup in this setup, which suggests the method is consistently useful, but the margin is small enough that these tests do not expose its full potential.

  • Across these workloads, suffix usually edges out prompt lookup rather than dominating it, which points to stable but moderate gains.
  • The small gap suggests the benchmark setting does not contain enough repeated structure for suffix matching to fully pay off.
  • This makes the current evaluation better for testing robustness and consistency than for showing the maximum upside of suffix decoding.

Systems Lesson

Acceptance must justify its cost

What matters is not acceptance by itself, but how much extra accepted work each method gets relative to the effort it spends to generate and verify proposals.

  • Draft-spec often reaches much higher acceptance than suffix, but its draft-model overhead is large enough that vanilla still beats it in most cases.
  • Suffix gains less acceptance, but it pays much less for each proposal, so the tradeoff is often better end-to-end.
  • The real metric is accepted progress per unit of extra work, not acceptance rate in isolation.

Known Shortcomings

Current limitations of tree-spec

Tree-spec shows promise, but the current system still has several engineering and evaluation issues that make its gains harder to interpret and reproduce consistently.

  • Tree-based verification is still coupled with suffix-decoding machinery, so the implementation is not yet cleanly isolated.
  • Performance is configuration-sensitive: local-only, local+global, and incremental-cache variants behave very differently.
  • Global cache often causes performance degradation instead of helping, especially as prompt count grows.
  • Tree verification gains are not yet consistently reliable across workloads and verifier designs.
